# How do autonomous drones navigate inside GPS-denied industrial facilities?

Industrial facilities are dense tangles of steel, concrete, and volatile materials where GPS signals never reach—yet these are precisely the places where routine inspection is most critical. From inside storage tanks to beneath offshore platforms, autonomous drones must localize with centimeter-level precision without satellite aid to capture actionable asset data. The answer lies in a tightly engineered stack of simultaneous localization and mapping (SLAM), multi-spectral sensing, and on-edge sensor fusion that builds a real-time understanding of the drone’s surroundings.

## What sensors do autonomous drones use to perceive their environment indoors?

A drone operating inside a GPS-dark environment relies on a combination of LiDAR, stereo cameras, ultrasonic rangefinders, and inertial measurement units (IMUs). No single sensor is sufficient; it is the fusion of these inputs that creates a robust, three-dimensional understanding of the space.

Tactical-grade LiDAR sensors—such as a 360° scanning unit with a 100 m range and ±2 cm accuracy—provide the primary geometric map. These units emit millions of laser points per second to generate a dense point cloud of walls, pipes, and structural members. High-resolution stereo cameras capture visual features like valve markings, corrosion stains, and weld seams, feeding visual odometry and later defect analysis. An onboard IMU, sampling at 1,000 Hz, tracks acceleration and angular velocity to bridge the gaps when visual features are sparse, while ultrasonic sensors handle close-range (<5 m) obstacle avoidance. This multi-layered perception suite ensures that even in dusty, low-light conditions inside a crude oil tank, the drone maintains full situational awareness.

## How does SLAM enable real-time mapping and localization without GPS?

Simultaneous Localization and Mapping (SLAM) builds a map of an unknown environment while simultaneously determining the drone’s position within that map by detecting and tracking distinct features. It effectively replaces the satellite constellation with an evolving, self-referenced coordinate system.

LiDAR-based SLAM algorithms, such as LOAM (LiDAR Odometry and Mapping) and its lightweight variants, process point clouds to extract edge and planar features. As the drone flies, these features are matched frame-to-frame across time, enabling pose estimation drift of less than 0.5% of distance traveled. When combined with visual SLAM techniques like ORB-SLAM3, the system achieves robustness in texture-rich areas and tighter loop closures. For example, during a 200-meter flight inside a 30-meter diameter fuel storage tank, a fused SLAM pipeline holds positional error below 5 cm even after multiple 360° rotations, without any global reference. Modern implementations run entirely onboard on an NVIDIA Jetson or similar edge computer, generating the map and localization updates at 30 Hz so the flight controller can react instantly.

## Can visual-inertial odometry replace GPS for precision flight?

Visual-inertial odometry (VIO) fuses camera images with high-frequency IMU data to estimate the drone’s motion in real time, providing a drift-resilient alternative to GPS that many flight controllers can consume directly as a position and velocity source.

VIO systems like VINS-Fusion or Google’s Cartographer use tightly coupled optimization to minimize error accumulation. The camera tracks hundreds of visual features across successive frames at 30–60 Hz, while the IMU provides rapid pose predictions between frames, compensating for temporary visual loss due to motion blur or darkness. The result is a localization stream accurate to within 0.1–0.3% of distance traveled—on a 100-meter inspection pass through a refinery pipe rack, that translates to a cumulative error of only 10–30 cm. In Solaris Robotics deployments, VIO serves as the primary fallback when LiDAR SLAM encounters geometrically degenerate spaces, such as long uniform corridors, ensuring uninterrupted mission execution. ## How do ultra-wideband (UWB) beacons improve reliability in large facilities?

When an inspection runs through kilometers of tunnels or across cavernous rooms where visual and LiDAR features are repetitive, pre-placed ultra-wideband (UWB) radio beacons anchor the drone’s position to a fixed global frame, eliminating long-term drift.

A network of lightweight, battery-powered UWB anchors placed at known survey points provides 10–30 cm absolute positioning accuracy at ranges up to 150 meters. The drone’s onboard tag measures time-of-flight to multiple anchors and trilaterates its position, updating at 20–50 Hz. Data from UWB is fused with the SLAM solution via a factor graph, correcting the local map in real time. This hybrid setup is particularly valuable for multi-year inspection programs in mines or large-diameter pipe galleries, where operators need exact repeatability: the drone returns to the same coordinate each time, aligning captures for trend analysis. ## Conclusion

Navigating inside GPS-denied industrial facilities demands a layered strategy—combining high-resolution LiDAR, visual-inertial odometry, SLAM engines, and optional UWB anchors into a single integrated autonomy stack. Each technology compensates for the weaknesses of the others, delivering the accuracy and reliability needed for safe, repeatable inspection. As edge computing advances and sensor costs continue to drop, these localization techniques will become standard across the industry, enabling fleets of autonomous drones to operate continuously inside the world’s most challenging infrastructures, capturing data that was simply impossible to reach before.
